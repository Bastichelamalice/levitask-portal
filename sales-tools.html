Traditional ergonomic chairs lock you in this posture, compressing your spine, restricting blood circulation and putting pressure on your lungs and organs. The result: back pain, muscle fatigue, and a loss of energy and focus. 80% of people experience this in their lifetime."

OUR SOLUTION

"Elevated Seating. A new seating concept that opens your body angle to 115°, halfway between sitting and standing. Elevated Seating decompresses your spine, frees your lungs, and improves blood circulation, giving you more energy and sharper focus throughout the day."

HOW IT WORKS

"The Vision Chair uses the T-Mech™, a patent-pending knee-pivot mechanism that tilts the seat forward. This forward tilt rotates the pelvis anteriorly, which naturally restores the spine's S-curve without any conscious effort from the user. And because the pivot point sits at the knee, the thighs stay in contact with the seat surface. No sliding. No discomfort."

THE RESULT

"Less pain. More energy. Deeper focus."

Discovery Questions — Uncover the Need
Questions to ask before pitching. The goal is to make the prospect articulate their own problem — then position the Vision Chair as the answer.
12 questions
Read

Don't pitch before you've listened. These questions surface pain before you present the solution — making the Vision Chair feel like an answer, not a sale.

Opening — establish context
1.How many hours a day does your team typically spend seated at their desks?
2.Do you have employees who've raised concerns about back pain or discomfort at work?
3.What's your current office chair setup — do you have a standard model or do employees choose their own?
Dig deeper — qualify the pain
4.Have you ever tracked absenteeism related to back or musculoskeletal issues?
5.When you bought your current chairs, what was the main decision criteria — price, aesthetics, certification, or something else?
6.How long ago did you last invest in ergonomic furniture — and what prompted that decision?
B2C — individual buyer
7.Do you experience any back or neck discomfort after a long day at your desk?
8.What do you currently do to manage that — lumbar pillow, standing desk, breaks?
9.If you could change one thing about how you feel at the end of your workday, what would it be?
Forward-looking — open the door
10.Is ergonomics something that comes up in your HR or wellness conversations?
11.Are you familiar with the concept of sitting posture being a root cause of back pain — not just a symptom?
12.If there was a solution that addressed the posture problem at its source, would that be worth exploring?

Rule of thumb: Ask 3–4 questions maximum before transitioning to the pitch. If the prospect mentions back pain, discomfort, or productivity loss unprompted — that's your signal to pivot.

The Buying Center — Know Your Stakeholders
In corporate sales, decisions are rarely made by one person. Map the 6 roles before your first meeting.
6 roles
View

Before walking into a corporate account, identify who plays each of these roles. The Decider and Buyer are rarely the same person who books your meeting. Missing a key stakeholder is the most common reason deals stall.

Initiator
The Trigger
The person who first identified the problem or suggested looking for a solution. Often an employee complaining about back pain, or an HR manager who read a report.
Ask: "Who first raised the idea of looking at ergonomic solutions?"
Influencer
The Advisor
Has no formal authority but shapes the decision. Could be an office manager, a trusted employee, or a consultant. Win them and they'll sell for you internally.
Ask: "Who else has strong opinions on workspace setup?"
Decider
The Decision Maker
Has final authority. In mid-size companies, usually the HR Director or COO. In larger companies, this may escalate to the CFO for significant purchases.
Ask: "Who gives final approval on investments like this?"
Buyer
The Purchaser
Handles the transaction — procurement, finance, or admin. Focused on price, terms, and process. Not usually interested in the product itself.
Ask: "Who handles the actual purchasing process?"
User
The End User
The person who will actually sit in the chair. Their buy-in matters — a pilot program that gets rave reviews from users will sell the deal faster than any presentation.
Ask: "Can we set up a demo for a few of the team members who'll use it?"
Gatekeeper
The Blocker
Controls access to decision makers. Often an assistant, office manager, or IT director who has to approve new vendors. Treat them with respect — they can kill your deal.
Ask: "Is there anyone I should loop in before we go further?"

The 2-question rule: 2–3 well-placed questions in the first 5 minutes will reveal the full structure. "Who else is involved in this decision?" and "Who gives final sign-off?" will map the buying center faster than any research.

5-Minute Demo Script
A structured walkthrough for in-person demos. Takes the prospect from curiosity to conviction in under 5 minutes.
5 steps
Read
1
Set the frame (30 sec)
Before they sit down
"Before you try it, I want to explain one thing. Most chairs try to make 90° sitting more comfortable. This chair eliminates the 90° problem. Sit however feels natural — don't try to sit 'correctly'."
2
Let them sit at 0° (30 sec)
Chair flat — T-Mech at neutral
"How does that feel? Notice where your back is — your lower back is probably rounding slightly. That's what happens at 90°."
Let them feel the baseline before activating Elevated Seating.
3
Activate the T-Mech (30 sec)
Ask them to slightly rise, then engage
"Now I'm going to lift the seat slightly — just rise a centimetre off the seat so the mechanism can move freely. Feel that? Your pelvis just rotated forward. Your lower back curve just came back — without any effort from you."
4
Let them explore (2 min)
Silent observation
"Take a minute — move around, recline, adjust the height. Notice how the backrest follows you automatically."
This is your most powerful minute. Don't talk. Let the chair sell itself.
5
The close question (30 sec)
After they stand up
"What did you notice? … [pause] … That's exactly what 115° does to the body. The question isn't whether it works — you just felt it. The question is: how many people in your [office / team / home] should feel that?"

Showroom Tips — Environment & Setup
How to set up your demo environment and behaviour to maximise conversion.
8 tips
Read
→Place the Vision Chair next to a conventional ergonomic chair. The contrast is your most powerful visual argument — let them sit in the conventional one first.
→Have the chair pre-set at a neutral position (T-Mech at 0°) so the activation moment is clear and dramatic.
→Keep a desk at working height nearby — people need context to feel how the posture changes during actual work.
→Don't demonstrate all adjustments upfront. Let them sit first. Adjustments come after they've felt the core benefit.
→For corporate visits, bring printed leave-behinds — the one-page spec sheet and ROI summary. Decision makers need something physical to share internally.
→When multiple people try the chair in a session, let each person adjust it themselves rather than adjusting it for them. The act of customisation builds ownership.
→If possible, leave a demo unit with a prospect for 1–2 weeks. Extended trial converts at significantly higher rates than single-session demos.
→After the demo, send a follow-up within 24 hours. Reference something specific they said during the sit. Personalisation signals that you were listening.
