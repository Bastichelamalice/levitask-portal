Quick Pitch

"The seating industry has relied for too long on one flawed principle: the 90° angle. The human body was never designed to sit at 90° for hours, it's a posture that fights your spine, not supports it. Levitask invented a new way to sit: Elevated Seating. The Vision Chair opens your hip angle to 115°, halfway between sitting and standing, restoring your spine's natural S-curve automatically, without any effort. Same price as a premium ergonomic chair. Fundamentally different result."

Structured Pitch

THE PROBLEM

"Most people sit 8 to 10 hours a day in 90° chairs. Traditional ergonomic chairs lock you in this posture, compressing your spine, restricting blood circulation and putting pressure on your lungs and organs. The result: back pain, muscle fatigue, and a loss of energy and focus. 80% of people experience this in their lifetime."

OUR SOLUTION

"Elevated Seating. A new seating concept that opens your body angle to 115°, halfway between sitting and standing. Elevated Seating decompresses your spine, frees your lungs, and improves blood circulation, giving you more energy and sharper focus throughout the day."

HOW IT WORKS

"The Vision Chair uses the T-Mech™, a patent-pending knee-pivot mechanism that tilts the seat forward. This forward tilt rotates the pelvis anteriorly, which naturally restores the spine's S-curve without any conscious effort from the user. And because the pivot point sits at the knee, the thighs stay in contact with the seat surface. No sliding. No discomfort."

THE RESULT

"Less pain. More energy. Deeper focus."

The Buying Center — Know Your Stakeholders
In corporate sales, decisions are rarely made by one person. Map the 6 roles before your first meeting.
6 roles
View

Before walking into a corporate account, identify who plays each of these roles. The Decider and Buyer are rarely the same person who books your meeting. Missing a key stakeholder is the most common reason deals stall.

Initiator
The Trigger
The person who first identified the problem or suggested looking for a solution. Often an employee complaining about back pain, or an HR manager who read a report.
Ask: "Who first raised the idea of looking at ergonomic solutions?"
Influencer
The Advisor
Has no formal authority but shapes the decision. Could be an office manager, a trusted employee, or a consultant. Win them and they'll sell for you internally.
Ask: "Who else has strong opinions on workspace setup?"
Decider
The Decision Maker
Has final authority. In mid-size companies, usually the HR Director or COO. In larger companies, this may escalate to the CFO for significant purchases.
Ask: "Who gives final approval on investments like this?"
Buyer
The Purchaser
Handles the transaction — procurement, finance, or admin. Focused on price, terms, and process. Not usually interested in the product itself.
Ask: "Who handles the actual purchasing process?"
Gatekeeper
The Blocker
Controls access to decision makers. Often an assistant, office manager, or IT director who has to approve new vendors. Treat them with respect — they can kill your deal.
Ask: "Is there anyone I should loop in before we go further?"
User
The End User
The person who will actually sit in the chair. Their buy-in matters — a pilot program that gets rave reviews from users will sell the deal faster than any presentation.
Ask: "Can we set up a demo for a few of the team members who'll use it?"

The 2-question rule: 2–3 well-placed questions in the first 5 minutes will reveal the full structure. "Who else is involved in this decision?" and "Who gives final sign-off?" will map the buying center faster than any research.

The Demo Script — Part 1: You Present
A step-by-step script for presenting the Vision Chair — based on how our co-founder Michael demos it.

1
Set the frame (30 sec)
"This is the Vision Chair — the world's first Elevated Seating device. Most ergonomic chairs try to make 90° sitting more comfortable. This chair eliminates the 90° problem entirely. Let me show you how."
Stay standing next to the chair. Don't sit yet — let them observe first.
2
Sit down — show the open hip angle (1 min)
"When I sit here, notice what happens automatically — my pelvis rotates forward, my spine finds its natural S-curve. No effort, no conscious posture correction. The relationship between my thigh and torso is now at 115–120°, which allows for proper blood circulation, relieves pressure from the organs, and lets the lungs breathe properly."
Point to your hip and pelvis as you explain. Pause and let them observe.
3
The knee pivot — your key differentiator (45 sec)
"What makes this truly unique is where the mechanism pivots — around the knee. Watch: when I recline all the way back, my feet stay flat on the floor and my thighs stay in contact with the seat. Try that with any other forward-tilt chair and your knees lift off. That's the patent."
Recline slowly to demonstrate. This is your strongest differentiator vs saddle chairs and kneeling chairs.
4
Stand up — show the adjustments (1 min)
"The chair accommodates users from 155 to 195cm through three simple adjustments — all one-time, set and forget. Backrest height and depth via this lever. Seat depth via this knob. And for taller users, a longer stem pipe."
Demonstrate each adjustment physically. Keep it simple — this is not a technical briefing.
5
The HAD cross-sell opportunity (30 sec)
"The Vision Chair is designed to work with height-adjustable desks. Because you're sitting higher, the desk needs to go up too — from the standard 75cm to 80–90cm depending on height. That's a natural cross-sell opportunity for you."
Frame this as a business opportunity, not a limitation.
6
Materials & warranty (30 sec)
"Aircraft-grade aluminum frame. Biodegradable TPU casters. 10-year warranty on the mechanism and structure, 3 years on the fabric. This is the most premium product in the category."
End on confidence. Let the quality speak.

The Demo Script — Part 2: They Experience
Guidelines for when the customer sits in the chair — what to observe, what to say, and how to guide their experience.
4 steps
Read
1
Hand over the chair (30 sec)
"Now it's your turn — sit however feels natural. Don't try to correct your posture."
Step back. Give them space. Don't hover.
2
Let them feel the baseline (30 sec)
"Notice where your lower back is right now."
Don't explain yet. Let them feel the 90° position before activating anything.
3
Activate the T-Mech (30 sec)
"Now — slightly rise off the seat, just a centimetre, so the mechanism can move freely. Feel that? Your pelvis just rotated forward. Your lower back curve just came back — without any effort from you."
Guide their hand to the lever if needed. Watch their face — the reaction usually happens in seconds.
⚠ Important: the T-Mech cannot engage under full body weight. Always ask the customer to slightly rise off the seat before activating the lever — even just 1–2cm is enough.
4
Let them explore and close (2 min)
"Take your time — move around, recline, adjust the height. Notice how the backrest follows you automatically."

Then after 1–2 minutes: "What did you notice? … [pause] … That's exactly what 115° does to the body. The question isn't whether it works — you just felt it. The question is: how many people in your team should feel that?"
Don't talk during the exploration. Let the chair sell itself. The close question lands best after silence.

Showroom Tips — Environment & Setup
How to set up your demo environment and behaviour to maximise conversion.
6 tips
Read
→Have a height-adjustable desk available for the demo — the Vision Chair is designed to work with one, and showing the combination live is your strongest cross-sell argument.
→Place the Vision Chair next to a conventional ergonomic chair. The contrast is your most powerful visual argument — let them sit in the conventional one first.
→Have the chair pre-set at an elevated position (T-Mech™ at 12–14°) in your showroom. The forward tilt is visually striking next to conventional chairs — it creates curiosity and draws people in before you say a word.
→For corporate visits, bring printed leave-behinds — the one-page spec sheet and ROI summary. Decision makers need something physical to share internally.
→When multiple people try the chair in a session, let each person adjust it themselves rather than adjusting it for them. The act of customisation builds ownership.
